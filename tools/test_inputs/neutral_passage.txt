Making a good cup of coffee starts with the beans. You want beans that were roasted recently — within the last two or three weeks is ideal. Once they sit much longer than that, the oils that give coffee its flavor begin to break down. Buying whole beans and grinding them right before you brew makes a noticeable difference, even if you use a cheap grinder.

The grind size matters more than most people realize. A coarser grind works better for a French press, where the water and coffee sit together for several minutes. A finer grind is better for espresso, where the water passes through quickly under pressure. For drip coffee, something in between is about right. If your coffee tastes bitter, the grind is probably too fine. If it tastes thin or sour, it's probably too coarse.

Water temperature is another thing worth paying attention to. Boiling water is actually too hot — it scorches the grounds and pulls out harsh flavors. Somewhere around 195 to 205 degrees Fahrenheit is the sweet spot. If you don't have a thermometer, just let the kettle sit off the heat for about thirty seconds after it boils.

The ratio of coffee to water affects strength. A common starting point is about two tablespoons of ground coffee per six ounces of water, but this is just a baseline. Some people prefer it stronger, some weaker. It's worth experimenting until you find the ratio that works for you.

Cleaning your equipment regularly also makes a difference. Coffee oils build up inside grinders and carafes over time, and they go stale. That staleness transfers to the next pot you brew. A quick rinse isn't always enough — occasional deeper cleaning with a small amount of dish soap, or a dedicated coffee cleaner, keeps everything tasting fresh.

None of this has to be complicated. Once you get a feel for the variables, making a decent cup becomes automatic. The goal is just to understand what each adjustment does so you can fix it when something tastes off.
